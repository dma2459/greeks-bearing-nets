        {\Large \textbf{I. INTRODUCTION}}
        \vspace{0.5em}

        \textbf{A. The Fundamentals of Options}\\
        An option is a financial derivative that gives the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a specified "strike" price on a specific date. Our project focuses exclusively on \textit{European Call Options}. A call option gives the buyer the right to \textit{buy} the asset. The "European" designation means the option can \textit{only} be exercised exactly on its expiration date, unlike American options which can be exercised early. This constraint simplifies the boundaries of valuation, making them the standard benchmark for pricing theories.
        \vspace{0.5em}

        \textbf{B. The Importance of Pricing}\\
        Calculating the exact fair value of these contracts at the End-of-Day (EOD) close is critical to global finance. Market makers rely on fair-value models to quote bid-ask spreads safely. Furthermore, clearinghouses (like the OCC) and mutual funds rely on hyper-accurate EOD valuations to calculate overnight margin requirements and Net Asset Values (NAV). Mispricing risk at the EOD can lead to catastrophic, systemic financial contagion.
        \vspace{0.5em}

        \textbf{C. The Black-Scholes Baseline}\\
        For fifty years, the standard has been the Black-Scholes formula (1973). However, it relies on flawed assumptions: that asset returns follow a log-normal distribution and that volatility is constant. In reality, markets exhibit "fat tails" (frequent extreme crashes) and volatility clustering. We hypothesize that deep neural networks can bypass these parametric constraints, learning the true, non-linear pricing function directly from historical data.
        \vspace{1.2em}

        {\Large \textbf{II. METHODOLOGY}}
        \vspace{0.5em}

        \textbf{A. TimeGAN Data Synthesis}\\
        Training on a single historical timeline causes severe overfitting. We address this with a Time-series GAN (TimeGAN), which jointly trains a generator, discriminator, and an embedding network to preserve the temporal dynamics of real market data. Each synthetic sample consists of a 60-day window of per-step features: log-return ($r_t$), rolling 20-day realized volatility ($\sigma_t$), time-to-expiry ($\tau$), and strike-normalized spot price ($S_t/K$). These features are drawn from the same distribution as real equity data, ensuring the generated paths exhibit the volatility clustering and fat tails observed in practice.
        \vspace{0.5em}

        \textbf{B. Feature Engineering and Input Structure}\\
        Each training sample is structured as a sequence of length 60 fed into the Transformer encoder. At each step $t_i$, the input vector contains: log-return, realized volatility, moneyness ($S_{t_i}/K$), and time-to-expiry ($\tau_i = T - t_i$). The sequence is aligned so the final step always corresponds to the option's valuation date, giving the model the correct spot price $S_T$ and $\tau = 0$ at the moment of pricing.

    \end{column}

    \begin{column}{.31\linewidth}

        \textbf{C. Architecture and Output Formulation}\\
        The encoder consists of 4 multi-head self-attention layers (8 heads each), enabling the model to dynamically attend to distant volatility regimes within each 60-day window. This is something a feedforward network cannot do. A linear readout head projects the final-step encoder representation to a scalar. To enforce the no-arbitrage lower bound and prevent negative prices, the model predicts the option's \textit{time value} in log space. The final price is recovered as:
        \begin{equation*}
            \text{Price} = \text{expm1}(\hat{y}) + \max(0,\, S_T - K)
        \end{equation*}
        where $\hat{y}$ is the raw scalar output and $\max(0, S_T - K)$ is the intrinsic value floor.
        \vspace{1.0em}

        {\Large \textbf{III. RESULTS}}
        \vspace{0.5em}

        \textbf{A. Generative Data Validation (TimeGAN)}\\
        The TimeGAN reproduces the non-Gaussian character of real SPY log-returns. Both distributions display excess kurtosis well above the Gaussian baseline of 3.0 (real: 7.66, synthetic: 5.14), confirming the generator captures fat-tail behavior. The synthetic peak is slightly sharper and the far-tail mass slightly thinner—the GAN modestly under-generates extreme events—but the overall shape alignment is close enough that paths sampled from the generator make a faithful proxy for the true return distribution at training time.

        \textbf{B. Pricing Accuracy vs.\ Black-Scholes}\\
        On the held-out test set, the mixed-label Transformer (C) achieves the lowest overall MAE (\$1.51) and is the only neural model to beat the Black-Scholes baseline (\$1.52). The Heston-trained Transformer (A) is essentially tied with BS at \$1.55, while the pure-GAN variant (B) lags noticeably at \$2.15. The pattern is structural: GAN paths alone are too noisy to teach reliable pricing, but pairing them with Heston-priced contracts gives the network both distributional realism and the no-arbitrage rigor a closed-form formula already provides.

        {\Large \textbf{IV. DISCUSSION}}
        \vspace{0.5em}

        \textbf{A. Numerical Stability}\\
        A critical design challenge was preventing the network from producing arbitrage-violating negative prices. Early experiments with direct price regression suffered from exploding gradients when spot prices moved far from the strike. Predicting the \textit{time value} in log space—and recovering the full price via $\text{expm1}(\hat{y}) + \max(0, S_T - K)$—resolved this entirely. The intrinsic value floor acts as a hard constraint, and the log transformation compresses the output range, making training significantly more stable and convergent.
        \vspace{0.8em}

        \textbf{B. EOD Scope and Intraday Limitations}\\
        Our model is fundamentally designed around the structure of EOD data: a fixed 60-day lookback window with one observation per trading day. This makes it well-suited for overnight margin and NAV calculations, but not for intraday pricing. Intraday option values are driven by tick-level microstructure—order flow imbalances, bid-ask dynamics, and real-time volatility surfaces—that daily features cannot capture. Extending this approach to intraday applications would require a complete re-architecture around higher-frequency inputs and much shorter sequence horizons.
